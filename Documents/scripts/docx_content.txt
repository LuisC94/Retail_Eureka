Package 2, (WP2) - Use Cases, Requirements and Preliminary Studies, represents an important phase of the RETAILL project (REtail using Technology based on Artificial InteLLigence). Its main purpose is to establish the conceptual, technical, and methodological framework that will guide the development of the RETAILL solution. WP2 ensures that subsequent design and implementation activities are grounded in real operational contexts, validated user needs, and robust technological principles.WP2 provides the necessary bridge between the strategic vision defined in WP1 (Project Management and Coordination) and the technical development activities that will follow in WP3, WP4, and WP5. It combines the definition of use cases, requirements analysis, and preliminary studies with the definition of the system’s overall architecture and detailed development protocol. In this way, WP2 ensures coherence between the project’s innovative objectives, particularly the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into retail logistics and sustainability, and the practical realities of industrial partners.The present deliverable, D2.2 - Report on Solution Architecture and Detailed Project Development Protocol, documents the baseline architecture of the RETAILL solution, describing the main hardware and software components, their interactions and interoperability mechanisms. In addition, it outlines the security, ethical and privacy principles embedded in the system design. Finally, the deliverable defines a detailed development protocol, including methodologies and validation procedures for each main system module. This protocol guides the subsequent implementation, integration, and refinement activities to be carried out in later phases of the project.Hardware and Software Architecture2.1 Overview This architectural design is conceived to underpin a sophisticated and dynamic food supply chain for fresh food products, considering the specific case of apples and kiwis, encompassing all stakeholders from producers to consumers. The primary objective is to achieve end-to-end visibility, proactive quality control, and maximized operational efficiency through intelligent, real-time decision-making.2.1.1  Architectural PrinciplesThe system's foundation is built upon critical principles to ensure the reliability, adaptability, and cross-border functionality required by the RETAILL consortium (Portugal, Spain, Turkey).Modularity: The architecture is structured as a network of microservices, ensuring that each AI Agent and analytical component can be developed, deployed, and updated independently without disrupting the entire chain.Interoperability: This is a fundamental principle, achieved by establishing a FIWARE-compliant Data Space and utilizing DLT/Blockchain technologies for immutable traceability records. This ensures secure and standardized data exchange.Scalability: The infrastructure and software are designed to elastically scale, accommodating significant fluctuations in demand, an increasing number of producers, and the high-volume data streams generated by tracking and lifecycle sensors.Security & Data Sovereignty: The system ensures data privacy and sovereignty through anonymization and access validation within the Data Space. DLT/Blockchain protocols provide immutability and reliable traceability of data, which is essential for establishing trust in the supply chain.2.1.2  AI-Driven Core and Real-Time ManagementThe central intelligence is a collective of specialized AI Agents, each focused on a specific objective within the supply chain, as defined by RETAILL's goals:Order Management AI Agent: Responsible for integrating retailer orders and optimizing the allocation of specific batches of fresh food products (in this case, apples/kiwis) based on real-time quality status provided by the Lifecycle Agent, prioritizing product rotation to minimize waste.Stock Management AI Agent: Manages not just quantity but optimizes the location and movement of stock based on quality prediction, suggesting reordering in warehouses to minimize spoilage and maximize remaining shelf life.Routing AI Agent: Calculates the optimal route (going beyond simple shortest-path optimisation) by incorporating critical variables such as cold-chain temperature data, real-time traffic conditions, and weather-related disruptions (e.g., heavy rain, extreme heat, storms) that may affect travel time or product quality. The agent also incorporates sustainability factors, such as fuel efficiency and CO₂ emissions, ensuring that route choices balance operational efficiency, product freshness, and environmental impact.Tracking AI Agent: continuously monitors and validates the physical movement of product batches and their in-transit environmental conditions (e.g., temperature, humidity) throughout the logistics chain. The agent ensures traceability and data integrity and provides validated tracking data to other system modules.Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) AI Agent: computes environmental performance indicators (e.g., carbon footprint and other LCA-based metrics) for each product supply chain using data aggregated across the producer-to-retail boundary. The LCA Agent applies standardised methodologies aligned with ISO 14040/14044 and is designed as a modular model that links agricultural production, post-harvest conditioning and packing, distribution logistics, and retail cold-chain handling.Lifecycle AI Agent: This is a predictive model, using data from environmental (e.g., O₂, CO₂, T°, Humidity) and advanced Machine Learning models to calculate the deterioration and the Remaining Shelf Life (RSL) or total lifetime of every batch of apples/kiwis across the entire chain.Critical Events AI Agent: Acts as an immediate alarm system, reporting anomalies (accidents, sensor failures, temperature spikes) to all other agents. Its swift action is designed to minimize the impact on product quality and enable immediate corrective action.Multi-criteria Decision AI Agent: Provides structured decision support by evaluating and comparing alternative options based on multiple criteria. The agent is designed to combine decision-relevant indicators, originating from system modules, external sources, or predefined datasets, with retailer-defined business, sustainability, and product-related criteria.The program’s engine will orchestrate efficient communication between agents and supply chain stakeholders.2.1.3  High-Level DiagramsThe high-level diagrams depict the logical architecture of the system, distinct from its data flow. The system comprises multiple entities that interact continuously. System management is handled by a pool of specialized single-purpose agents. The system is designed so that when a profile associates with an entity, the corresponding agent is automatically invoked.Figure 1 illustrates the system's entities. Upon creation, a Stock Management Agent is embedded within the Producer, establishing connections with three auxiliary agents: AI Lifecycle, AI Tracking, AI Lifecycle Assessment, and AI Routes.Figure 2- Instantiation of entities with the Producer profile.2.2Hardware2.2.1 IoT and sensorsThe RETAILL project utilizes both monotonic and non-monotonic sensors to provide the rich, continuous data streams required for the AI Agents.Monotonic Sensors (Quality Focus):Devices: Temperature probes, Relative Humidity (RH%), O₂, CO₂, and C₂H₄ (Ethylene).Location: Installed in Controlled Atmosphere (CA) chambers in warehouses and within transport containers/trucks. They are essential inputs for the Lifecycle Agent's deterioration prediction models.Non-Monotonic Sensors (Logistics/Impact Focus):Devices: GPS Devices and Accelerometers (Gyroscopes).Location: Installed in carrier vehicles for real-time tracking (feeding the Route and Tracking Agents) and for monitoring mechanical shocks that could physically damage the product.Sensor availability and deployment considerationsIt is important to note that sensor availability may vary across pilots. The RETAILL platform is therefore designed to gracefully adapt to heterogeneous sensing configurations, using baseline sensors where only minimal instrumentation is available, while progressively exploiting richer sensor data in more advanced deployments.2.2.2 Local infrastructures vs. edge.The architecture implements a hybrid model to balance low-latency alerts with centralized, complex processing.Local Infrastructures: IoT gateways, devices installed in warehouses and vehicles that collect sensor data (temperature, HR%, etc) locally. Performs pre-processing and noise filtering on sensor data. Crucially, it runs light anomaly detection algorithms locally to trigger instant alerts (e.g., a critical temperature spike) without relying on cloud latency, ensuring rapid response to critical product events.Centralized Computing: The Proof of Concept (PoC) solution will be developed using a client/server architecture implemented with microservices. This architecture allows for easy scalability to support numerous clients or agents in real-world scenarios. 2.2.3 Servers, storage, network.This section details the traditional infrastructure components.Servers and Processing:AI Agents: Executed within containers to ensure portability and horizontal scalability (ability to increase agent instances under high load).ML Models: Require dedicated GPUs or TPUs for the efficient retraining of complex prediction and optimization models.Storage:Databases: SQL and vector search databases.Blockchain Storage: The distributed storage mechanism ensures the immutability of traceability records.Network:Connectivity: Utilizes Virtual Private Networks (VPNs).2.3 Software2.3.1 Data layer (databases, ETL).This layer is responsible for managing and processing all data generated by sensors and the transactions of the AI Agents.Transactional Databases, like PostgreSQL, are reliable, robust, and open source for structured data like orders, stock levels, and user accounts (handled by the Order Management and Stock Management Agents).ETL/Data Pipeline: in the initial development phase, and to manage operational costs before scaling, the ETL pipeline logic will be implemented using standard Python libraries. This architecture allows rapid development and agility.Real-Time Data Extraction (E): Real-time sensor data from the Edge infrastructure will be pushed to a RESTful API Endpoint. This endpoint will trigger the Critical Events AI Agent to perform immediate data validation and anomaly detection, crucial for quality control. Python with the Requests library will be used for data transmission from the Edge, and a microservices framework will be used to receive and process the data stream.Batch Data Transformation (T): For scheduled daily cleaning, aggregation, and preparation of historical data for the Lifecycle Agent, transformation logic will be executed using the pandas library. Data Loading (L): The transformed data, which includes cleansed historical records and final decisions, will be loaded into the central data persistence layer.2.3.2 Business layer (ML/AI models, analytical services).This layer contains the system's intelligence and business logic, where the AI Agents reside.For AI Agent Runtime, Python will be used with Microservices Framework. Python is the industry standard for ML model development, and a microservices approach ensures that each Agent can be developed and scaled independently (modularity principle).For ML/AI Frameworks, TensorFlow/PyTorch and Scikit-learn will be used. These provide the necessary libraries for building complex models to predict product deterioration and optimize logistics.2.3.3 Presentation layer (dashboards, interfaces).This layer provides the multi-device, user-friendly interfaces required by all stakeholders to consume the intelligence generated by the AI Agents.For Front-end Web Platform (Dashboards), Django Template Language (DTL) is used to render HTML on the server, combined with native HTML5, CSS3, and Vanilla JavaScript for structure, styling, and interactivity.For Data Visualization, sophisticated real-time Dashboards will be created presenting complex actionable insights, including Critical Event Alerts and the Product Freshness Index, derived from the AI Agent outputs.2.3.4 APIs and integration services.This component ensures secure, controlled communication between all internal systems and external partner platforms, leveraging the DLT mechanisms defined in the architecture.API Protocol: RESTful APIs and WebSockets, where REST handles standard data exchange between agents and WebSockets are used for instantaneous communication of critical alerts and real-time data streaming (e.g., from the Critical Events Agent to the Dashboards).Integration Services: Standard Python Database Connectors and DLT Connectors that facilitates the reliable transfer of data between the various data components and integrates the DLT/Blockchain layer into the transactional flows for immutability and traceability.2.4 Interoperability and integrationInteroperability is a cornerstone of the RETAILL platform, essential for connecting the distributed AI Agents and the systems of the diverse partners across Portugal, Spain, and Turkey.2.4.1 Mechanisms for integration between partners.The platform employs a multi-layered approach to ensure secure data exchange and functional integration across all components.DLT/Blockchain technologies are specifically integrated to preserve the integrity and traceability of product-related data throughout the entire chain, to create a foundation of trust and an immutable record, which is fundamental for data sovereignty among partners.API Microservices or RESTful: The solution will be developed based on a microservices or RESTful architecture, where communications between the various components and agents are defined by protocols specifically tailored to the information required for agent decision-making.2.4.2. Communication protocols and data standardization.Data flow and standardization are critical given the heterogeneity of partners and data sources.IoT Communication Protocol: a communication protocol will be defined across the various IoT devices to standardize incoming data, ensuring the system remains brand-agnostic. Data Standardization and Quality: the platform implements strict standardization to ensure the interoperability and comparability of data from diverse sources, including monotonic (e.g., T°, RH%) and non-monotonic sensors (gyroscopes).Open Access & Third-Party Integration: the project will develop an Open Data Backbone with open interfaces, facilitating the technology-agnostic coupling of third-party applications and algorithms that ensures the platform's flexibility and allows for the expansion of its ecosystem.Security, Privacy and Ethics3.1 SecurityThe RETAILL security framework is designed to safeguard all data exchanged within the consortium and to ensure compliance with European regulatory standards. Access to system components follows a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) model, ensuring that users are granted only the permissions strictly required for their responsibilities across retail, logistics, production and analytical modules.Data integrity is preserved through the use of hashing mechanisms, version control, and immutable audit logs, providing full traceability of data flows, model updates and user actions. This ensures accountability, supports incident analysis and strengthens trust in the system’s outputs.To protect data confidentiality, the architecture applies encryption in transit (TLS 1.3) for all communications and encryption at rest (AES-256) for databases, model repositories and storage services. Encryption keys are managed through secure vaults aligned with EU best practices. Together, these measures establish a robust and scalable security foundation for the development, integration and future deployment of all RETAILL components.Blockchain ensures security by creating an immutable, tamper-proof record of data integrity, while maintaining privacy through permissioned access protocols that restrict visibility to authorized partners only.3.2 PrivacyA Data Management Plan (DMP) has been defined to ensure the responsible collection, processing, storage, and sharing of all project-related data. The DMP details the types of data to be gathered, the purpose of their use, and the technical and organisational measures that guarantee compliance with ethical and legal standards. It also establishes clear data retention policies and defines procedures for secure data disposal after the project’s conclusion.To ensure data protection, anonymization and pseudonymization techniques are going to be applied to any dataset containing personal or sensitive information. These techniques guarantee that no individual or organization can be identified directly or indirectly without explicit consent. Whenever possible, aggregated or synthetic data will be used to support AI model training and validation, minimizing privacy risks.The RETAILL platform fully adheres to the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR - Regulation (EU) 2016/679) and national data protection frameworks. All partners have appointed data protection focal points to ensure alignment with GDPR principles, including lawfulness, fairness, transparency, data minimization, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity, and confidentiality. Access to personal or restricted data follows role-based authorization (RBAC) and is continuously logged to ensure accountability and traceability across the system.3.3 Ethics PrinciplesEthical oversight is formally ensured through the submission of the project to the Ethics Committee of the School of Health of the Polytechnic of Porto (ESS | P.PORTO). This committee evaluates data collection, consent procedures, privacy safeguards, and risk mitigation measures involving human or sensitive data. Particular attention will be given to the organisation of focus groups and usability tests with the RETAILL system, ensuring that all participant interactions are conducted ethically, with informed consent and under controlled conditions. Continuous ethical monitoring will be maintained during the project to ensure compliance with approved protocols and to integrate evolving ethical standards and societal expectations. The project will adopt the principles of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) to promote interpretability and user trust. All AI models will include documentation of decision logic and model provenance, ensuring that outcomes can be understood, audited, and contested when necessary. Detailed development protocol4.1 Life Cycle Assessment4.1.1 Purpose and ScopeThe LCA and ML-enhanced LCA module aims to provide robust environmental indicators that support operational, tactical, and strategic decisions within RETAILL. The overall purpose of this module is to develop an advanced LCA system aligned with ISO 14040/14044, capable of delivering timely and decision-relevant environmental indicators (including carbon footprint) for fresh food products and supporting supply-chain optimisation within the RETAILL platform. 4.1.2 Decision Context within RETAILLThe LCA and ML-enhanced LCA module is designed to provide quantitative environmental indicators that can be embedded into RETAILL decision workflows, complementing IoT-based monitoring and AI-driven planning. Within the RETAILL architecture, the module will contribute to: (a) product-level environmental performance monitoring (baseline and trends); (b) hotspot identification and prioritisation of improvement actions; (c) scenario testing (e.g., alternative routes, packaging options, cold-chain settings, supplier choices); and (d) multi-criteria decision support when coupled with other modules (e.g., MCDA and routing).4.1.3 Methodological ApproachThe methodological framework follows the principles and requirements of ISO 14040/14044. For the current baseline iteration, the LCA modelling adopts a cradle-to-gate system boundary – from the producer up to retail –, so that distribution logistics and cold-chain operations can be assessed and improved. Functional unit (FU): 1 kg of fresh fruit (kiwi or apple), 'as obtained/as sold', delivered to retail under defined handling conditions. The kiwi and apple assessments will be developed as independent studies, not intended for public comparative assertions.Foreground processes included: (1) agricultural production; (2) field emissions associated with fertilisation (e.g., N₂O, NH₃, NO₃⁻), and other relevant elementary flows; (3) post-harvest operations at packing/conditioning; (4) distribution and transport to retail; (5) cold-chain storage (e.g., packing/coolstore and retail refrigeration). Background processes (e.g., upstream production of inputs such as fertilisers, pesticides, packaging materials; fuel and electricity upstream) will be incorporated using secondary datasets, while maintaining traceability and documentation of assumptions.4.1.4 Decision Criteria FrameworkThe RETAILL LCA decision criteria framework organises environmental indicators in a concise set of ReCiPe 2016 midpoint impact categories, selected for their relevance to fresh fruit supply chains from producer to retail and for their direct link to controllable operational drivers within RETAILL:1) Emissions and pollutants (air, water and soil related impacts)Climate change (GWP100) – kg CO₂-eq/kg - captures greenhouse gas emissions associated with the main energy- and nitrogen-related hotspots of the system, namely electricity consumption, fuel combustion in agricultural machinery and transport, and direct N₂O emissions from nitrogen fertilisation.Terrestrial acidification – kg SO₂-eq/kg - reflects emissions primarily linked to fertiliser application and soil management in agricultural production, as well as to combustion processes in field operations and transport activities.Eutrophication – kg P-eq/kg - represents nutrient enrichment potential caused by phosphorus and nitrogen losses from fertiliser use, irrigation, and runoff/leaching processes, which are key environmental pressures in irrigated fresh fruit systems.These indicators support route optimisation and supplier evaluation.2) Energy, water and resource use (scarcity-related impacts)Fossil resource scarcity – kg oil-eq/kg - captures dependence on fossil energy carriers used in electricity generation, agricultural machinery and transport, supporting the evaluation of energy efficiency and alternative fuel or routing scenarios.Water consumption – m³/kg - reflects irrigation requirements.Mineral resource scarcity – kg Cu-eq/kg - accounts for the use of mineral resources embedded in agricultural inputs and packaging materials, supporting longer-term resource efficiency considerations.This structured set of midpoint indicators ensures that the dominant emission pathways and resource pressures of fresh fruit supply chains, fertiliser management, irrigation and energy use, logistics and cold-chain operations, are consistently captured and can be directly linked to RETAILL decision processes such as hotspot identification, scenario testing and multi-criteria decision analysis4.1.5 Validation and Stakeholder InvolvementValidation within the RETAILL LCA framework is interpretability of preliminary LCA results and identified hotspots.Focus groups will support iterative refinement of data templates, clarification of operational practices and identification of missing or underestimated flows.Cross-Module ValidationA key validation step consists of testing the usability and consistency of LCA outputs when integrated into other RETAILL modules. Planned cross-module validation includes:MCDA: use of LCA indicators in supplier and scenario sustainability scoring;Routing: assessment of emission-aware routing and logistics optimisation based on LCA-derived transport parameters;Order Management: evaluation of scenario-based planning where environmental indicators complement quality and availability constraints;Lifecycle Agent: qualitative and quantitative consistency checks between environmental performance, storage conditions and product ageing dynamics.This integrated validation ensures that LCA results are not only technically robust but also operationally meaningful, directly supporting real-time and strategic decision-making within the RETAILL platform.4.1.6 Expected OutputsThe LCA module will deliver several technical and processes.4.2 Stock management4.2.1 Purpose and ScopeThis section is vital for minimizing food waste and maximizing profitability by adjusting stock levels based on product quality conditions. Effective stock management is a critical aspect of this project; ensuring the right quantities are on hand not only boosts profits and reduces costs but also minimizes waste, leading to positive environmental outcomes. The scope of this module concludes with the final evaluation of laboratory prototyping and real-world pilot testing.4.2.2 Decision Context within RETAILLThe Stock Management Agent is responsible for managing and maintaining the quantity and location of goods at every point in the supply chain. Its primary objective is to optimize inventory and prevent shortages or overstocking. To achieve this, it operates within a high-stakes decision context where biological spoilage and logistical variables must be balanced to ensure operational viability and effectiveness.4.2.3 Methodological ApproachThe methodology is structured into two main phases. The first phase involves defining the critical variables required for decision-making. The second phase focuses on studying and selecting appropriate algorithms for the system. This includes testing classical algorithms, such as Genetic Algorithm (GA), Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), or Simulated Annealing (SA), alongside the implementation of Reinforcement Learning (RL) techniques. Through these combined approaches, the system addresses the complex aspects of stock management and optimizes operations according to the specific objectives defined for each profile.4.2.4 Decision Criteria FrameworkThe decision-making process is governed by a vector of essential variables optimized by the algorithms, such as, inventory and demand (stock levels, re-order frequency, and demand forecasts); product status (freshness index, quality and lead times); logistics and risk (logistics scores and transport risk scores); and strategic alignment (sustainability scores and compliance metrics). The system prioritizes quality and efficiency as the decisive factors in the optimization process.4.2.5 Validation and Stakeholder InvolvementA comprehensive validation plan is established at the beginning of the laboratory prototype phase, focusing on two main scenarios:Realistic Environment Testing: The agent operates in conditions designed to closely mimic real-world stock and production objectives.Extreme Scenario (Stress) Testing: The agent is tested under extreme sales variance and production disruptions to evaluate its resilience at operational limits.Collaboration: Partners use Python for implementation, GitHub/GitLab for version control, and Teams for communication.4.2.6 Expected OutputsOptimized Inventory Policy: A robust set of parameters that balance availability with waste minimization.Reduced Spoilage: Direct contribution to the project’s goal of minimizing product wear and environmental impact.Operational Viability: A performance assessment that ensures the stock policy is effective in a real-world environment.4.3 Multicriteria decision making4.3.1 Purpose and ScopeThis section defines the protocol for the development, validation, and integration of a multicriteria decision-making (MCDA) model within the RETAILL platform. The objective is to support complex operational decisions in fresh food supply chains by systematically balancing different decision criteria.The protocol establishes a structured approach to identify relevant decision criteria, select appropriate MCDA methods, validate the decision framework with stakeholders, and ensure its seamless integration with other RETAILL modules, namely Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and logistics routing.4.3.2 Decision Context within RETAILLMCDA framework will be designed to support retailers in the evaluation, selection and/or prioritisation of suppliers of fresh and perishable food products. Retailers must regularly decide which suppliers to maintain, prioritise, or exclude, based on multiple and often conflicting criteria.In this context, each supplier represents a decision alternative, while the evaluation criteria are derived from different dimensions, such as economic, environmental, operational, social, and quality-related. These criteria are informed by data integrated and/or generated within the RETAILL platform, such as LCA and logistics. The MCDA framework aggregates this multi-dimensional information into transparent and interpretable outputs, such as supplier rankings or risk scores, enabling retailers to make structured, data-driven, and sustainability-oriented supplier management decisions.4.3.3 Methodological ApproachThe development of the MCDA framework follows a structured methodology composed of four main phases:1)     Identification of decision criteria grounded in scientific literature and project requirements.2)     Structuring criteria into coherent decision dimensions.3)     Selection and formalization of the MCDA model.4)     Validation and refinement through tests with the use case and focus group sessions.4.4.4 Decision Criteria FrameworkDecision criteria will be structured into a set of main dimensions (e.g. Economic/Financial; Environmental/Sustainability; Social; Quality; Operational/Logistical; and Technological/Information Systems. This structure ensures a comprehensive and balanced evaluation of suppliers, reflecting both operational performance and sustainability objectives. A preliminary taxonomy of criteria is defined based on project requirements and literature, and is iteratively refined as pilot data and operational feedback become available.The MCDA tool is designed to be configurable by the end user, allowing retailers to: 1) define and adjust the weights of the criteria according to their strategic priorities; 2) select which criteria are included in the decision model. This flexibility ensures that the decision framework can be adapted to different retail contexts, supplier portfolios, and sustainability strategies.4.4.5 MCDA Model Selection and StructureBased on the characteristics of the decision problems and data availability, a small set of suitable MCDA methods is identified through a review of the relevant scientific literature and best practices in supplier evaluation and sustainability-oriented decision-making.Candidate methods are subjected to preliminary tests using representative use cases and pilot/simulated data, allowing an assessment of their suitability in terms of transparency, computational efficiency, interpretability, and ease of integration with the RETAILL platform.The final MCDA model is selected based on these criteria, ensuring robustness, practical applicability, and alignment with the needs of retail decision-makers.4.4.6 Validation and Stakeholder InvolvementThe MCDA framework will be validated through expert focus group sessions, involving end users and technical-scientific stakeholders. Feedback collected is used to refine the criteria relevance and outputs.4.4.7 Expected OutputsValidated multicriteria decision framework;Formal taxonomy of decision criteria;Technical specification for system integration;Decision workflows supporting pilot implementation.4.4 Product tracking4.4.1 Purpose and ScopeThis module is essential for ensuring supply chain transparency and providing real-time (or right-time) location and environmental condition data for all other AI Agents. The scope includes the continuous monitoring of the product from the plantation to the final consumer, ensuring that all environmental and logistical data is captured accurately.4.4.2 Decision Context within RETAILLThe Tracking Agent functions as the primary data provider for the RETAILL ecosystem, continuously monitoring and validating product conditions, such as temperature, humidity, ethylene exposure, shocks and location, in real time. This information is essential for the Lifecycle Agent to maintain accurate predictions of product aging and remaining shelf life, and for the Routing Agent to make informed transport decisions that optimize delivery performance, preserve product quality, reduce operational risks, and support sustainability objectives. The Tracking Agent effectively establishes the “digital birth certificate” of each batch, enabling the integration of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) data to compute environmental KPIs across the supply chain.4.4.3 Methodological ApproachThe methodology is designed to manage the integration of physical infrastructure (IoT sensors) and the digital trust layer (DLT/Blockchain) to ensure data integrity from the source. The development focuses on Task 3.3 (Product tracking) and Task 2.4 (Security) to implement the immutable record system. The Tracking Agent is created in Python to receive and process continuous data streams.4.4.4 Decision Criteria FrameworkThe tracking framework is built upon the collection and processing of specific real-time variables that feed the decision-making process:Environmental Sensors: Monitoring of O2, CO2, C2H4, Temperature (℃), and Relative Humidity (%).Physical Status: Location data to determine product wear.Integrity Metrics: DLT/Blockchain records that guarantee the trustworthiness and immutability of traceability data.4.4.5 Validation and Stakeholder InvolvementA comprehensive testing and validation plan is established to ensure system reliability across several phases:DLT/Blockchain Integrity Test: Validating the robustness of the system to ensure recorded data (temperature, location) cannot be altered after registration.Agent Reliability Tests: Testing data collection in extreme environments and the agent's ability to filter noise and outliers.Integration Testing: Validating the communication between tracking modules and other AI Agents.4.4.6 Expected OutputsImmutable Traceability Records: A secure, DLT-backed ledger of every environmental event the product was subjected to from plantation to consumer.Right-Time Traceability: Real-time visibility of all relevant parameters for all stakeholders.Operational Readiness: A functional and validated tracking version capable of supporting large-scale pilot applications and ensuring product integrity since "birth".4.5 Order management4.5.1 Purpose and ScopeThe Order Management Agent is responsible for predicting demand, generating optimal replenishment orders, and handling real-time order adjustments based on inventory policies. This agent functions by bridging the gap between market requirements and supply chain execution, ensuring that the replenishment process is proactive rather than reactive.4.5.2 Decision Context within RETAILLWithin the RETAILL ecosystem, this agent coordinates the start of each logistical movement by processing order requests and initiating transport. It operates as a dual-capability engine, requiring both predictive algorithms to anticipate market needs and optimization algorithms to determine the most effective response based on the inventory policies established by the Stock Management Agent.4.5.3 Methodological ApproachThe methodological approach integrates advanced machine learning techniques with optimisation methods to support accurate and adaptive order management. The development of the module is structured around two core components:Demand Forecasting: Deep learning models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN), and transformer-based architectures are used to predict customer demand with high temporal accuracy. These models incorporate seasonality patterns, promotional effects, holiday periods, weather conditions, and other external drivers to generate robust short- and medium-term forecasts.Order Generation and Policy Optimisation: Forecast outputs are combined with inventory policies to compute the optimal order quantity and timing. Classical optimisation methods and metaheuristics are applied initially to ensure stability and interpretability. In later stages, a Reinforcement Learning (RL) module will be introduced to allow the system to adapt dynamically to evolving operational conditions, retailer profiles, and uncertainties in demand and supply. The RL component enables continuous learning from real-world interactions, improving order efficiency and responsiveness over the system’s operational lifetime.4.5.4 Decision Criteria FrameworkTo find the optimal replenishment vector, the agent processes several critical categories of data:Historical & External Data: Extensive sales history, seasonal patterns, and external variables like holidays or extreme weather.Predictive Metrics: Forecasted demand generated by the deep learning models.Optimization Goals: The objective is to maximize a defined function that balances stock availability against the logistics and quality constraints provided by other RETAILL agents.4.5.5 Validation and Stakeholder InvolvementThe testing and validation plan is established to assess performance across three primary areas during the laboratory prototype and pilot phases:Forecast Accuracy: Performance is measured using standard metrics (MAE, RMSE) to ensure improvement over legacy systems and responsiveness to volatility.Objective Function Validation: Both real-world and extreme edge-case data are used to compare the agent's actual results against theoretical optimal values.RL Analysis: Testing the learning rate and adaptation capability of the RL module across diverse scenarios.Technical Milestones: The development roadmap moves from data cleaning and baseline forecasting to laboratory prototyping and a final real-world pilot evaluation to verify order generation efficiency.4.5.6 Expected OutputsAccurate Replenishment Orders: Generation of final order quantities that minimize the risk of shortages or overstocking.Operational Integration: Secure communication of order quantities to retail partners' systems via RESTful APIs.Adaptive Intelligence: A self-improving system through the RL module that adapts more effectively to specific supply chain profiles over time.4.6 Route Management4.6.1 Purpose and ScopeThe Route Management module is responsible for generating optimal or near-optimal logistics routes for fresh and perishable food products, taking into account travel distance, fuel consumption, delivery time windows, perishability dynamics, and environmental impact. Its scope includes the design, development, and integration of an intelligent routing engine that supports the RETAILL ecosystem, enabling logistics operators and retailers to plan efficient, sustainable, and spoilage-aware transport operations. This protocol defines the methodological principles guiding module development, validation procedures, and the supporting tools for implementation.4.6.2 Decision Context within RETAILLThe Routing Agent operates at the core of logistics optimisation within RETAILL. It receives order requests from the Order Management Agent, product quality and spoilage information from the Lifecycle Agent, and environmental indicators from the LCA Agent.The routing problem addressed includes multi-stop vehicle routing with constraints such as:perishability decay models for fresh products;strict delivery time windows;vehicle capacity limitations;cold-chain compliance;multi-objective optimisation (cost, CO₂, food loss);dynamic disruptions (traffic, delays, condition fluctuations).By integrating these constraints, the Route Management module determines the most efficient and sustainable routing plans, directly influencing the global performance of the fresh-food supply chain.4.6.3 Methodological ApproachThe development methodology combines classical optimisation, metaheuristics, and, later in the project, reinforcement learning for adaptive decision-making. The main steps include:1) Route Network ModellingConstruction of a weighted logistics graph including distances, real travel times, storage nodes, and dynamic attributes derived from sensors and tracking.2) Problem FormalisationTranslation of operational needs into a Dynamic Vehicle Routing Problem (DVRP) variant:VRP with Time Windows (VRPTW);Capacitated VRP (CVRP);Perishable VRP (PVRP);Multi-objective VRP for cost-emissions-waste minimisation.3) Algorithmic DesignA hybrid routing engine is developed using:Metaheuristics (Genetic Algorithms, Tabu Search, Simulated Annealing, Hill Climbing);Heuristics (Nearest Neighbour, Clarke & Wright);Multi-objective optimisation frameworks;Future extension: Reinforcement Learning to support dynamic re-routing.4) Integration with RETAILL Data ArchitectureAPIs allow continuous data exchange with:Order Management (demands);Tracking (real-time conditions);LCA (environmental scoring);Lifecycle Agent (remaining shelf life predictions).4.6.4 Routing Decision Inputs and ConstraintsThe Route Management module relies on a structured set of inputs that define the operational, product-related, environmental, and business constraints relevant for computing feasible and efficient routes. These inputs do not duplicate the environmental criteria already detailed in the LCA module but instead summarise the decision elements directly required by the routing engine to perform optimisation.Operational and Logistical InputsThese are the fundamental elements that shape the physical feasibility of routing plans:Distances and estimated travel times between locations.Fleet characteristics (vehicle type, refrigeration capacity, load limits).Delivery time windows imposed by retailers or distribution centres.Warehouse and depot constraints (opening hours, loading/unloading policies).Product Quality and Perishability InputsDerived from the Lifecycle and Tracking Agents, these inputs ensure that routing decisions account for biological constraints:Remaining shelf life forecasts for each batch.Sensitivity to temperature, humidity, shocks, or other environmental deviations.Real-time updates on product condition during transport.Environmental Performance Inputs (from LCA)The routing engine incorporates only the necessary indicators from the LCA module to enable sustainability-aware optimisation:Estimated emissions per kilometre or per vehicle type.Environmental penalties associated with food loss along a route.Comparative sustainability scores for alternative logistics scenarios.Business and Economic ConstraintsThese elements support cost-efficient planning and service-level compliance:Transportation cost parameters (fuel, labour, tolls, vehicle wear).Service-level requirements such as on-time delivery rates.Contractual or operational penalties related to delays or disruptions.Together, these inputs form the decision boundary within which the routing engine operates, enabling it to generate routes that are operationally feasible, quality-preserving, cost-efficient, and consistent with the sustainability objectives of RETAILL.4.6.5 ValidationThe validation of the Route Management module follows a multi-layered process designed to ensure that the routing engine is accurate, robust, and aligned with real operational needs. Stakeholder involvement, particularly logistics managers, drivers, and use-case partners, is essential to guarantee that the developed functionalities reflect practical constraints and create measurable value across the supply chain.Algorithmic ValidationThe first stage of validation assesses the optimisation engine using controlled datasets and classical benchmarks. Its functionality depends on and contributes to a shared decision ecosystem where routing, forecasting, tracking, and sustainability assessments are interconnected. The integration ensures that routing decisions are not computed in isolation but are dynamically informed by real-time operational data, environmental indicators, and product-condition forecasts.The Routing Agent will be fully embedded within the RETAILL decision ecosystem, receiving key inputs from multiple agents to generate feasible, efficient, and quality-preserving routes.
